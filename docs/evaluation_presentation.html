The Problem

A tedious, high-stakes
data entry task

Railway maintenance teams receive scanned equipment manuals as image-only PDFs. Every part on every page must be entered into a maintenance management system — by hand.

Current State
  • Operator opens manual, opens spreadsheet, types each row one by one
  • 2–4 hours per 100 pages of manual
  • Typos in part numbers cause ordering errors downstream
  • Any part missed means a gap in the maintenance system
What We Want
  • Upload a PDF, get a structured spreadsheet back
  • Every part captured accurately, every time
  • Operator role shifts from data entry to review and sign-off
  • Repeatable across any equipment manual in the fleet
Why It's Not Simple

Scanned manuals are harder
to read than they look

Standard document tools struggle with these files. Three structural problems make automated extraction difficult.

⊞
Two columns per page

Parts tables are split into left and right columns. Text-reading software reads top-to-bottom and mixes the two columns together — producing garbled output that's difficult to untangle.

◧
Most pages have no data

Roughly 80% of pages are full-page diagrams with no parts data. Any approach must reliably skip these without accidentally missing real data pages.

🔤
Part numbers corrupt easily

Industrial part numbers like 0-3582001-0-18 are fragile. Standard text-recognition software confuses O/0 and l/1 constantly — enough to make a part number unrecognizable.

The two-column layout turned out to be the decisive problem. Any approach that reads text linearly — without understanding the visual layout — fails here.

The Breakthrough

Two things changed everything

1 · Structured output format
Tell the AI exactly what shape the answer must take

Earlier approaches let the AI produce free-form output — sometimes a table, sometimes prose, sometimes missing rows entirely with no warning.

The breakthrough was defining a strict output format upfront. Now if the AI produces something that doesn't match, the system flags it immediately rather than silently accepting a bad result.

Errors become visible — not invisible
2 · AI reads the PDF natively
No conversion step — AI sees the page like a human does

Instead of converting to images or text first, the AI reads the PDF directly — understanding layout, column structure, and which pages are diagrams vs. data.

This is what eliminates the two-column problem. The AI sees the full visual layout and extracts left and right columns correctly without any special configuration.

Two-column layout handled automatically
Both Approach 4 (third-party app) and Approach 5 (built in-house) use these same two principles. They achieve identical accuracy — the difference is how they're deployed.

How it achieves the speed

The plugin splits the 189-page manual into 10 sections and reads all 10 simultaneously — like having ten people read different chapters of the same book at the same time. Each section finishes in about 3–4 minutes; the whole manual is done when the slowest section finishes.

What the operator does

Run one command. The plugin handles extraction, filters out diagram pages automatically, and writes the result directly to Excel. The operator's job is to review the output — not produce it. Estimated review time: 10–15 minutes per manual.

Key Learnings

What this project taught us
about AI and messy data

👁
AI needs to see the layout, not just the text

The moment we stopped trying to convert the document into text first — and let the AI read it like a human would — accuracy jumped dramatically. Layout context is not optional for this type of document.

📋
Define the output format before you start

When the AI produces free-form output, errors are silent — a missing row looks the same as a row that was never there. A strict output format makes failures explicit and reviewable rather than invisible.

⚡
Parallelism is the speed lever

Running ten sections simultaneously compressed the extraction time from ~40 minutes (sequential) to ~4 minutes. For high-volume work, this is the difference between a tool the team actually uses and one they avoid.

🧑‍💼
The human role shifts, not disappears

Every approach that tried to remove the human entirely fell short. The better framing: AI handles extraction, human handles sign-off. That's a role the operator can do in 10–15 minutes instead of 2–4 hours.

Broader Takeaway

When does AI actually help
with messy, unstructured data?

This project is a good case study for a common question: when a document has structure that a human can clearly see, but no tool can reliably parse — where does AI fit?

AI works well when…
  • The document has a visual structure a human can recognize (tables, columns, sections)
  • The content is repetitive and predictable — same format across many pages
  • An occasional error is catchable in review — a human spot-check is viable
  • The goal is to shift human effort from production to review, not eliminate it
AI struggles when…
  • You need 100% guaranteed completeness with no human check whatsoever
  • The document format is highly variable across instances (AI tuned for one format breaks on another)
  • Errors have high downstream cost and there's no practical review step
The right question to ask
"What is the smallest human touch that makes AI output trustworthy enough to use?"

For this project: an operator reviews a flagged summary — not the whole PDF. That 10–15 minute review step is what makes a 100%-accurate result reliable in practice.

The answer to that question is different for every workflow. Finding it is the real design challenge — not building the AI model.
